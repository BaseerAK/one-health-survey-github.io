Comprehensive Review of Visual Analytics Systems for Emerging Infectious Disease Detection and Management: An Integrated One Health Perspective

Abstract

This systematic review analyzed existing systems and dashboards that use One Health datasets for infectious disease management, focusing on detection, prediction, planning, mitigation, and monitoring. It categorized these systems by their purpose, diseases addressed, data types (human, animal, environmental), and user groups, such as public health officials and researchers. The tasks performed include data collection, analysis, visualization, and decision-making. Visual analytics techniques range from interactive filtering to predictive modeling, with varying levels of user interactivity. The paper also classified these systems by paper type (e.g., case studies, reviews) and notes whether functional systems are available online. This systematic review aimed to examine the existing One Health system for the last 10 years (2015–2024). Through the search strategy, using the keywords "One Health Dashboard Visualization System OR One Health Dashboard OR One Health System." in Google Scholar, 132 articles were assessed in October of 2024. Based on the analysis, the primary objective of the research papers is monitoring, which accounts for 53% of the total. In contrast, the areas of prediction/forecasting and mitigation/response are less emphasized, each constituting only 6%. A significant majority of the systems focus specifically on COVID-19, representing 51% of the papers analyzed. When examining the data types utilized, integrated data derived from both human and animal sources is the most prevalent, comprising 30%. Combinations of various data types demonstrate differing levels of integration, with 10% integrating human, animal, and environmental data; most of these papers are categorized as review articles. Public health officials make up the primary user group at 72%, whereas veterinarians comprise the smallest group at 13%. The predominant task identified in the analysis is data analysis, which constitutes 93%, while collaboration plays a more supportive role at 18%. The most frequently used visualizations in the papers are graphs and charts, appearing in 69% of them, whereas hierarchical charts are represented in just 4%. Moreover, techniques such as machine learning and artificial intelligence are employed in 38% of the papers, with statistical and analytical methods, alongside data integration and processing, being less predominant. Additionally, we found that systems are more prevalent than dashboards, with 72% of the papers including systems compared to 28% of dashboards. Among the dashboards evaluated, only 16% provide high interactivity, while the majority offer low to moderate interactivity, comprising 46%.
